ABSTRACT

White dwarfs are the dense stellar remnants of Sun like stars that have run out of nuclear fuel and leave behind a core mainly composed of carbon and oxygen, with the majority having a mass of about 0.6M☉ and a radius similar to that of Earth (Space.com, 2024). Recent discoveries of exoplanets orbiting white dwarf stars have highlighted their potential to support a habitable environment and sparked interest about whether these systems could maintain conditions that would be suitable for organisms to develop.

In this work, we review current understandings of the formation, stability, and habitability of planets orbiting white dwarfs in order to evaluate their potential for life. We examine the physical constraints affecting planetary survival, including the impact of stellar evolution, the location and evolution of habitable zones as white dwarfs cool and fade, and the biological and observational aspects for detecting life. While conditions for habitability are restrictive, emerging evidence suggests white dwarf systems are interesting targets in the search for extraterrestrial life.

✧ 1. INTRODUCTION ✧

White dwarfs mark the final evolutionary stage of stars with initial masses of around 3-4M☉. Approximately 10 billion exist in the Milky Way (Space.com, 2024), with around 97% of stars in our galaxy eventually becoming white dwarfs (Scientific American, 2024). After exhausting their nuclear fuel, these stars shed their outer layers, leaving behind compact, electron degenerate cores (Sentinel Mission, 2024). Without ongoing nuclear fusion, white dwarfs emit leftover thermal energy and ultimately become black dwarfs.

Some white dwarfs are surrounded by debris disks, which can form second generation planets, while others retain pre-existing exoplanets that survived the host star's evolution (Universe Today, 2024). These findings have raised growing interest in whether white dwarf systems can sustain conditions suitable for life, this paper explores both the theoretical and observational perspectives.

✧ 2. PLANETARY FORMATION & SURVIVAL ✧

When a parent star evolves into a red giant, it can engulf nearby planets within roughly 1 AU, meaning most close in planets are destroyed during this phase (Astronomy.com, 2024), however some research indicates that planets orbiting within approximately 2 AU may survive this red giant stage. Although, their orbits can expand due to stellar mass loss, resulting in increased semimajor axes and longer orbital periods (Physics World, 2024).

Certain second generation white dwarf planets can form from a progenitor's debris disk, such as WD 1856+534 b (arXiv, 2024), happening through tidal disruption where a companion star is pulled apart by the white dwarf's strong gravity, producing a disk of gas and dust. Additionally, gravitational interactions like Kozai-Lidov cycles can affect distant planets, drawing them closer to the white dwarf (IOP Science, 2024a).

✧ 3. HABITABLE ZONES ✧

Due to their small radii and low luminosities, white dwarfs emit very little light (Embry-Riddle, 2024), with their only significant source of energy being residual thermal radiation which decreases over time. Consequently, their habitable zones, defined as regions where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface (Oxford Academic, 2024), are found extremely close to the star, typically around 0.012AU (IOP Science, 2024b).

As white dwarfs cool, their habitable zones migrate inward, the rate depending on the star's age and temperature (IOP Science, 2024b). Planets within the habitable zone are likely to become tidally locked, resulting in permanent day and night hemispheres, however a sufficiently dense atmosphere or active oceanic circulation could help redistribute heat effectively and maintain moderate surface conditions.

From Table 1, we can observe that white dwarf habitable zones are extremely close in comparison to those of main sequence stars (Kasting et al., 1993), resulting in orbital periods measured in days rather than years and increasing the likelihood of tidal locking.

✧ 4. THE HABITABILITY CHALLENGE ✧

This continuous inward migration of the habitable zone creates a significant problem. Even if a planet is initially located within this zone, it will eventually move out as the star cools and become too cold to sustain life. However, recent research suggests that in some cases the cooling of white dwarfs slows down due to neon-22 distillation, where heavier elements sink towards the core and release gravitational energy (University of Washington, 2024). This can temporarily heat the star and pause cooling for up to 10 billion years, prolonging the stability of the habitable zone (NASA Space News, 2025).

Newly formed white dwarfs can reach surface temperatures of over 100,000 K and emit strong ultraviolet and X-ray radiation (Number Analytics, 2024), posing a serious threat to nearby planets because it can strip away atmospheres and evaporate surface water (University of Washington, 2024). Therefore, planets must migrate into the habitable zone only after the white dwarf has cooled significantly, those with large enough water reserves and orbits which are beyond the typical habitable zone may be more likely to retain surface water (Astronomy & Astrophysics, 2014).

✧ 5. DETECTION & CHARACTERISATION ✧

Standard exoplanet detection methods, such as transit and radial velocity techniques, have proven to be less effective for white dwarf systems due to their small radii and low luminosities, with only 20% of known planets orbiting white dwarfs being discovered using these methods (IOP Science, 2024b).

These characteristics also make infrared excess detection more efficient. Using this approach, the first confirmed transiting white dwarf planet, WD 1856+534b, was discovered with JWST's Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI) detecting its thermal emission and confirming that it is close orbiting, Jupiter sized planet (Universe Today, 2024).

Direct imaging is another promising technique, benefitting from the white dwarf's low luminosity, which is typically 10^2-10^5 x fainter than main-sequence stars. This greatly increases the planet to white dwarf contrast ratio and improves detectability of orbiting planets (Astronomy & Astrophysics, 2014).

✧ 6. DISCUSSION ✧

The possibility that white dwarfs could host habitable planets challenges traditional beliefs about stellar life cycles and post main sequence environments, the significant physical and biological challenges, particularly regarding planetary migration, tidal locking and atmospheric survival, make chances of habitability seem unlikely. However, the long lifespan of white dwarfs and the ability to detect close in planets, make these systems essential targets for astrobiology. If life can emerge or adapt under such extreme and persisting conditions, white dwarf systems might be able to harbour life long after main sequence stars have faded.

✧ 7. CONCLUSIONS ✧

While prospects for life on planets around white dwarfs are uncertain, they are not negligible. Surviving or newly formed planets could maintain stable environments for billions of years under specific conditions. The discovery of transiting planets around white dwarfs indicates that such systems exist and merit attention in future observational campaigns, which may reveal whether these remnants of stellar evolution can host life.
