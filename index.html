Abstract

White dwarfs are the dense stellar remnants of Sun-like stars that have run out of nuclear fuel and leave behind a core mainly composed of carbon and oxygen. Recent discoveries of exoplanets orbiting white dwarf stars highlight their potential to support a habitable environment. In this work, we review current understandings of the formation, stability, and habitability of planets orbiting white dwarfs in order to evaluate the potential for life. We examine the physical constraints affecting planetary survival, the location and evolution of habitable zones as white dwarfs cool and fade, and the biological and observational aspects for detecting life. While conditions for habitability are restrictive, emerging evidence suggests white dwarf systems are interesting targets in the search for extraterrestrial life.

1. Introduction

White dwarfs mark the final evolutionary stage of stars with initial masses of around 3–4M☉, which is not enough for them to become neutron stars or black holes. There are approximately 10 billion white dwarfs in the Milky Way (NASA, 2023) and around 97% of all stars in our galaxy will eventually become white dwarfs (Scientific American, 2021). After exhausting their nuclear fuel, these stars shed their outer layers and leave behind compact, electron-degenerate cores (NASA, 2023). Without ongoing nuclear fusion, white dwarfs emit leftover thermal energy and gradually cool, ultimately becoming black dwarfs.

Some white dwarfs are surrounded by debris disks, which can form second-generation planets, while others retain pre-existing exoplanets that survived the host star's evolution (Sentinel Mission, 2023). These findings have raised growing interest in whether white dwarf systems can sustain conditions suitable for life. This paper explores both theoretical and observational perspectives on the habitability of white dwarf systems.

2. Planetary Formation and Survival

When a parent star evolves into a red giant, it can engulf nearby planets within roughly 1 AU (Universe Today, 2023), meaning most close-in planets are destroyed during this phase. However, some research indicates that planets orbiting within approximately 2 AU may survive this red giant stage (Astronomy.com, 2022), although their orbits can expand due to stellar mass loss, resulting in increased semimajor axes and longer orbital periods.

Certain second-generation white dwarf planets can form from a progenitor's debris disk, such as WD 1856+534 b (Physics World, 2024). This happens through tidal disruption, where a companion star is pulled apart by the white dwarf's strong gravity, producing a disk of gas and dust. Additionally, gravitational interactions like Kozai–Lidov cycles can affect distant planets, drawing them closer to the white dwarf (arXiv, 2024).

3. Habitable Zones around White Dwarfs

Due to their small radii and low luminosities, white dwarfs emit very little light (IOP Science, 2024), with their only significant source of energy being residual thermal radiation which decreases over time. Consequently, their habitable zones, regions where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface (MNRAS, 2022), are found extremely close to the star, typically around 0.012 AU (ERAU Portfolio, 2023).

As white dwarfs cool, their habitable zones migrate inward, with the rate of this migration depending on the star's age and temperature (IOP Science, 2024). Planets within the habitable zone are likely to become tidally locked, resulting in permanent day and night hemispheres (IOP Science, 2024). However, a sufficiently dense atmosphere or active oceanic circulation could help redistribute heat effectively and maintain moderate surface conditions across the planet.

4. The Habitability Challenge

The continuous inward migration of the habitable zone creates a significant problem. Even if a planet is initially located within this zone, it will eventually move out as the star cools, becoming too cold to sustain life. However, recent research suggests that in some cases, the cooling of white dwarfs slows down due to neon-22 distillation, where heavier elements sink towards the core and release gravitational energy (University of Washington, 2013). This can temporarily heat the star and pause cooling for up to 10 billion years, prolonging the stability of the habitable zone (NASA Space News, 2025).

Newly formed white dwarfs can reach surface temperatures of over 100,000 K (NASA Space News, 2025) and emit strong ultraviolet and X-ray radiation (Number Analytics, 2023), posing a serious threat to nearby planets, as such radiation can strip away planetary atmospheres (University of Washington, 2013) and evaporate surface water. For this reason, planets must migrate into the habitable zone only after the white dwarf has cooled significantly. Planets with large enough water reserves and orbits which are slightly beyond the typical habitable zone may be more likely to retain surface water (A&A, 2014).

5. Detection and Characterisation Opportunities

Standard exoplanet detection methods, such as transit and radial velocity techniques, have proven to be less effective for white dwarf systems due to their small radii and low luminosities, with only 20% of known planets orbiting white dwarfs being discovered using these methods.

Nonetheless, these characteristics can also offer unique advantages. The featureless spectra of white dwarfs make infrared excess detection more efficient. This approach enabled the discovery of the first confirmed transiting white dwarf planet, WD 1856+534b with JWST's Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI) detecting its thermal emission and confirming that it is close-orbiting, Jupiter-sized planet.

Direct imaging is another promising technique, benefitting from the white dwarf's low luminosity, which is typically 102–105 times fainter than main-sequence stars. This greatly increases the planet-to-white-dwarf contrast ratio and improves the detectability of orbiting planets (Universe Today, 2023).

6. Discussion

The possibility that white dwarfs could host habitable planets challenges traditional beliefs about stellar life cycles and post-main-sequence environments. With significant physical and biological challenges, particularly regarding planetary migration, tidal locking and atmospheric survival, the chances of habitability may seem low. However, the long lifespan of white dwarfs and the ability to detect close-in planets make these systems essential targets for astrobiology. If life can emerge or adapt under such extreme and persisting conditions, white dwarf systems might be able to harbour life long after main-sequence stars have faded.

7. Conclusion

While prospects for life on planets around white dwarfs are uncertain, they are not inconsiderable. Planets that survive the red giant phase or form afterward could maintain stable, habitable environments for billions of years under specific conditions. The discovery of transiting planets around white dwarfs indicates that such systems exist and merit attention in future observational campaigns, which may ultimately reveal whether these remnants of stellar evolution can host living worlds.
